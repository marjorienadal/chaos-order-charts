Hormuz: Four Clocks

Physical constraints that run on their own schedule

Clock 01
Tanker Backlog
900+ vessels stuck in or around the Gulf. Clearing the queue requires sequential transits through a constrained corridor. This is a logistics problem, not a diplomatic one.
85–110 days minimum
Clock 02
War Risk Insurance
Cargo rates at 3.75% of insured value vs 0.1% pre-war. Joint War Committee has not delisted the area. Insurers move on operational reality, not ceasefire announcements.
6–8 weeks minimum
Clock 03
Rerouting Costs
Cape of Good Hope adds 16–32 days and ~$930k in fuel per voyage. Hapag-Lloyd: no normal network for at least six to eight weeks even if ceasefire holds.
6–8 weeks minimum
Clock 04
The Mines New
Laid haphazardly by small boats with no central coordination. No complete mapping. Some have drifted. Neither Iran nor the US has rapid mine clearance capability. Iran's FM confirmed: "technical limitations."
Unknown timeline

The paper market trades ceasefire probability. The physical market trades four clocks that run on their own schedule.
They don't converge on an announcement. They converge when the clocks stop.
